Predicting Credit Card Approval¶

By Daniel Heffron, Jainam Patel, and Sash Sujith

Introduction¶

Credit card approval is a task that requires consideration of multiple factors. A manual approval process would be menial, time consuming, and inefficient overall. With this project, our group is trying to create a process to automate the credit card approval process.

We are looking at a dataset from Kaggle.com that contains two CSV files. The first file (application_record.csv) contains personal information about applicants. The second file (credit_record.csv) contains the credit records of applicants. We will examine the association between 1) factors such as age, number of children, income, etc. and 2) approval. We will also create our own model to predict whether or not an applicant is approvable or not. The goal of the project is to find the factors that influence the credit card approval decision the most.

Then, we read in the data from the credit_record.csv. There are 7 possible values for the Status column. A 0 denotes a balance that is 1-29 days past. 1 is 30-59 days past due. 2 is 60-89 days overdue. 3 is 90-119 days overdue. 4 is 120-149 days overdue. 5 is overdue or bad debts, write-offs for more than 150 days. A C means paid off that month and an X is no loan for the month.

For the data from credit_record.csv, we realized that the credit history for each applicant varied quite a bit. Some applicants had a credit history of 4 months, while others had upwards of 15 months. To make the data more readable, we decided to find an average status for each applicant.

app_train, app_test, credit_train, credit_test = ms.train_test_split(new_app, new_credit, random_state = 1)

Accuracy Score: 0.4011775938892425

After testing our algorithm, we can see that we predicted the correct credit score from our applicant testing set with only ~40.12% of the time. While this level of accuracy seems poor, it's effectiveness is much higher relative to that of random guessing. Keep in mind that our algorithm must select from a pool of 8 possible integer values in the range [-2, 5]. This means chance of randomly guessing the right credit score for an applicant is 1 out of 8, or 12.5%. Therefore, our algorithm is statistically over 3 times more accurate than random guessing.

Now lets see how good it is at predicting approval rate. To do this we will map all values from [-2, -1, 0, 1] in credit_test and testing_results to 'approved', and all values from [2, 3, 4, 5] in credit_test and testing_results to 'not'.

Converting Age and Employment to Years¶

In the dataset, age is originally given as a negative number of days in the column "DAYS_BIRTH". The date of birth was determined by counting backward from the current day. For example, if an entry has a 0 in the "DAYS_BIRTH" column, it means they were born today (-1 would mean they were born yesterday).

In order to avoid any potential confusion, we decided to create a new column "AGE_YEARS" that would have the ages of every applicant in years (not days). To fill the new column, we took all the values in the original "DAYS_BIRTH" column and divided them by -365.

We do the same for the "DAYS_EMPLOYED" column, converting it to "YEARS_EMPLOYED".

Conclusion¶

Given the results of fitting our data in various linear regression models, we see that our hypothesis was wrong. It is evident that education has the greatest influence on the credit card approval of the five factors examined. This is not to say that the other factors (that werent explored with the same depth as age, income, amount of work experience, education, and marital status) are unimportant. From the linear discriminant analysis, we saw that including all the different factors yielded the correct approval prediction with 99.65% accuracy. While we understand that the real-world approach that credit card companies utilize to automate the approval process is much more thorough and complex, it was still an enlightening experience to figure out and replicate this process on a smaller dataset.
